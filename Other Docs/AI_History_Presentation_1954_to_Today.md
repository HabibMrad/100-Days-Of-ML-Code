# Slide 1 — Title
## The History of Artificial Intelligence (1954–Today)
- A 10-slide overview of major milestones, winters, and breakthroughs.
- Focus: from early symbolic AI to modern generative AI.

---

# Slide 2 — 1954–1959: Foundations and Early Optimism
- 1954: Georgetown-IBM machine translation demonstration showed early promise for language processing.
- 1956: Dartmouth Summer Research Project formally coined the term “Artificial Intelligence.”
- 1957: Rosenblatt introduced the Perceptron, one of the first trainable neural models.
- 1958: John McCarthy developed LISP, a foundational language for AI research.
- Main idea of the era: human reasoning might be encoded as symbolic rules.

---

# Slide 3 — 1960s: Symbolic AI Expands
- Researchers built programs for theorem proving, game playing, and basic reasoning.
- Notable systems: ELIZA (1966), SHRDLU (late 1960s/early 1970s work).
- Government and academic funding increased due to strong expectations.
- Limitation: systems performed well only in narrow, controlled settings.

---

# Slide 4 — 1970s: First AI Winter
- Progress in real-world tasks was slower than expected.
- 1973 Lighthill Report criticized AI progress in the UK.
- Funding cuts followed in several regions.
- Lesson: AI needed stronger methods, better data, and more compute.

---

# Slide 5 — 1980s: Expert Systems Boom (and Bust)
- Commercial AI resurged via expert systems (e.g., XCON at DEC).
- Knowledge-engineering became a major industry activity.
- Japan’s Fifth Generation project increased global AI competition.
- Late 1980s: high maintenance costs and brittleness caused another downturn.

---

# Slide 6 — 1990s: Statistical Machine Learning Rises
- Shift from hand-written rules to data-driven learning.
- Key methods: Bayesian networks, decision trees, SVMs, ensemble methods.
- 1997: IBM Deep Blue defeated world chess champion Garry Kasparov.
- AI matured through probability, optimization, and better benchmarks.

---

# Slide 7 — 2000s: Data + Compute Era
- Growth of the web produced massive datasets.
- GPUs and distributed systems enabled larger models.
- Speech recognition and computer vision improved steadily.
- 2006: “Deep learning” re-emerged with advances in multi-layer neural networks.

---

# Slide 8 — 2010s: Deep Learning Breakthroughs
- 2012: AlexNet won ImageNet by a wide margin, accelerating deep learning adoption.
- 2014–2016: sequence models and attention transformed language and translation.
- 2016: AlphaGo defeated Lee Sedol, showing strategic planning + learning power.
- Industry-scale AI deployment expanded in search, ads, recommendation, and perception.

---

# Slide 9 — 2020s: Foundation Models and Generative AI
- Transformer-based large language models became central to NLP.
- Generative AI spread to text, code, images, audio, and video.
- Multimodal systems combined language, vision, and speech.
- New priorities: alignment, safety, regulation, reliability, and energy efficiency.

---

# Slide 10 — Today and What Comes Next
- AI is now a general-purpose technology across science, business, and education.
- Current frontier: agentic systems, tool use, reasoning, and real-world autonomy.
- Open questions:
  - How to make AI trustworthy and verifiable?
  - How to distribute benefits broadly?
  - How to balance innovation with governance?
- Closing thought: AI history is cyclical—progress accelerates when ideas, data, and compute align.
